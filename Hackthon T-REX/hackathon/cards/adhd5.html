Definition

anxiety is that sense of feeling constantly ‘on edge’. Common symptoms include feeling like you can’t sit still; waking up in the morning with a very busy mind, and having a racing mind in the evening when you’d usually feel like winding down; feeling like you ‘should’ always be doing something; worrying more than usual, especially over situations you wouldn’t think too much about; and physically, you may feel more nervous, experience increased hunger or suppressed appetite, and be unable to relax.

Diagnosis

SYMPTOMS->
1. Restlessness: Feeling on edge or unable to sit still.
2. Irritability: Easily getting frustrated or agitated.
3. Muscle tension: Feeling tense or having muscle aches.
4. Fatigue: Feeling tired or lacking energy.
5. Difficulty concentrating: Finding it hard to focus on tasks or schoolwork.
6. Sleep disturbances: Having trouble falling asleep, staying asleep, or having restless, unsatisfying sleep.
7. Excessive worrying: Constantly thinking about what might go wrong or feeling apprehensive.
DIAGNOSIS->
Low-level anxiety in individuals may not necessarily lead to a formal diagnosis of an anxiety disorder, especially if the symptoms are mild and do not significantly impair their daily functioning. However, if someone is experiencing consistent anxiety symptoms that cause distress or interfere with their life, it's important to seek help from a mental health professional.
It's essential for a mental health practitioner to assess the individual's specific symptoms, medical history, and overall well-being to determine the appropriate diagnosis and treatment plan, if needed. Diagnosis is a complex process, and it cannot be solely based on a numerical point scale. Therefore, if you or someone you know is experiencing anxiety symptoms, it's best to consult with a healthcare provider or mental health specialist for a comprehensive evaluation and appropriate guidance.

Causes


1.Academic Pressure:
a.School performance expectations
b.Exams and tests
c.Homework overload

2.Personal Concerns:
a.Self-esteem issues
b.Body image concerns
c.Fear of failure

3.Media Influence:
a.Exposure to violent or distressing content in movies, TV shows, or online
b.Social media pressures and comparisons

Prevention


Foods to Eat


1.Fatty Fish (Salmon, Trout, Sardines) - Rich in omega-3 fatty acids, which are linked to reduced anxiety.
2.Nuts and Seeds (Almonds, Walnuts, Flaxseeds) - High in magnesium, which can help regulate emotions and reduce anxiety.
3.Leafy Greens (Spinach, Kale) - Packed with magnesium, which may help improve overall brain function and reduce anxiety.
4.Turmeric - Contains curcumin, which has been studied for its potential to reduce anxiety.
5.Dark Chocolate - Contains antioxidants and flavonoids that can improve mood.
6.Probiotic-Rich Foods (Yogurt, Kefir, Kimchi) - Support gut health, which is linked to brain health and may reduce anxiety.
7.Whole Grains (Quinoa, Brown Rice) - Provide a steady release of energy and contain magnesium, which can help reduce anxiety.
8.Chamomile Tea - Has calming effects and may reduce symptoms of anxiety.
9.Oranges - High in vitamin C, which can help lower stress hormones.
10.Turkey - Contains tryptophan, an amino acid that can promote serotonin production, leading to improved mood.

Medication


1.Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs):
a.SSRIs like fluoxetine (Prozac), sertraline (Zoloft), and escitalopram (Lexapro) are commonly prescribed for anxiety disorders in adolescents.
b.They work by increasing serotonin levels in the brain, which can help improve mood and reduce anxiety.
2.Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors (SNRIs):
a.SNRIs such as venlafaxine (Effexor) may be considered in some cases.
b.SNRIs increase the levels of both serotonin and norepinephrine in the brain, which can have anxiolytic (anxiety-reducing) effects.
3.Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT):
a.CBT is a type of therapy that is often considered a first-line treatment for anxiety disorders in young individuals.
b.It focuses on identifying and changing negative thought patterns and behaviors related to anxiety.
c.Mindfulness and Relaxation Techniques:

Exercises


1.Deep Breathing:
a.Sit or lie down in a comfortable position.
b.Inhale deeply through your nose for a count of four.
c.Hold your breath for a count of four.
d.Exhale slowly and completely through your mouth for a count of six.
e.Repeat for several breaths, focusing on the breath and calming the mind.
2.Child's Pose (Balasana):
a.Start on your hands and knees.
b.Sit back on your heels while reaching your arms forward on the ground.
c.Rest your forehead on the mat and breathe deeply.
d.Hold for a few minutes, focusing on relaxing your body and mind.
3.Legs Up the Wall (Viparita Karani):
a.Sit sideways with your right hip against the wall.
b.Swing your legs up onto the wall and lie on your back.
c.Rest your arms by your sides with palms facing up.
d.Close your eyes, breathe deeply, and relax for 5-10 minutes.
